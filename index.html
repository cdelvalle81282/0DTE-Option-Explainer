How a same-day options trade can cost less and put time on your side

"0DTE" just means zero days to expiration — an option you buy today that expires today, at the 4:00 p.m. ET close. We trade them on the S&P 500 index (SPX) using a strategy called the butterfly.

Why SPX

No shares change hands — just cash

Stock options and index options end their lives very differently. This is the single biggest reason we run these trades on SPX.

🍎 Stock options (e.g. Apple)
  • Expire into actual shares of stock
  • Forget to close before the bell? You could wake up owning (or owing) shares
  • You must babysit the position into the close
📈 SPX index options
  • Cash settled — no shares, ever
  • Hold to the close and any value is simply deposited as cash the next morning
  • No surprise assignments, no early exercise

If your SPX butterfly finishes in the money, the position just disappears and the money shows up in your account. One more perk worth a Google: tax rule 1256 may treat index-option gains favorably — ask a tax professional, not us.

The exit plan

Sell half at a double. Let the rest ride.

1
Buy 4 butterflies instead of 1 (so: buy 4 lower-wing puts, sell 8 body puts, buy 4 upper-wing puts).
2
Set a limit order to sell half at double what you paid. Paid $1.22? The order to sell 2 butterflies goes in at $2.44. If it hits, that's a 100% gain — and your original money is off the table.
3
Let the other half ride to the 4:00 p.m. close. You're now playing with house money. Anything it settles for is deposited as cash the next morning.
The bottom line: your maximum loss is always just what you paid to open the trade — often around $150 or less. Your upside if the index lands in the cash zone can be several times that. Low cost, defined risk, time on your side, and cash settlement. That's the 0DTE butterfly. 🦋
